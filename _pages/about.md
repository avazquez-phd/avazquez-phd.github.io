I am a postgrad research student in Philosophy at the University of Southampton. My PhD is on the relation between psychological language and machine behaviour. 

I work under the supervision of [Genia Schönbaumsfeld](https://geniaschonbaumsfeld.academia.edu/) and [Will Mcneill](https://philpeople.org/profiles/william-e-s-mcneill). 

Since 2019 I am an editorial assistant in the _Nordic Wittgenstein Review_.

# Publications

### Book chapters

'Wittgenstein, Psychological Language and Artificial Intelligence' (2024). In Brian Ball, Alice C. Helliwell, and Alessandro Rossi (eds.), _Wittgenstein and Artificial Intelligence, Volume I: Mind and Language_. Chapter 3. Anthem Press. [Published version.](https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/abs/wittgenstein-and-artificial-intelligence/wittgenstein-psychological-language-and-ai/CD66B9E88534ECED099FF1B400B2870F)

<details>
<summary>A B S T R A C T</summary>
This chapter argues that certain uses of psychological language in the context of artificial intelligence (AI) escape the weak-strong AI dichotomy and can be understood in terms of, or as sharing features with, what Wittgenstein called the ‘secondary’ sense, meaning, or use of concepts.
The first part of the chapter reconstructs the fundamental problem in the philosophy of AI, namely, ‘Can machines think?’, and shows that figurative and literal uses of psychological language relate to the weak and strong conceptions of AI, respectively.
In the second part, we elaborate on the chapter’s argument. First, we explore the conceptual nature of the problem of ‘thinking’ machines. We then briefly review some Wittgensteinian approaches to these issues. Wittgenstein scholars have mainly criticised strong or literal ascriptions of psychological predicates to machines, and seem to agree that only weak or figurative uses of psychological language in AI are unproblematic.
In the last section we introduce a descriptive use of psychological language in the science of AI. We study such a use of language based on Wittgenstein’s methodological requirement of attending to and describing language use to clarify conceptual confusion. While showing how this use of language is meaningful in a secondary sense, we distinguish the descriptive usage from both literal and figurative ascriptions of psychological predicates to machines.
One of the outcomes of this study is that the analysis of meaningful uses of psychological language in AI in terms of ‘secondary use’ helps show that the problem ‘Can machines think?’ might give rise to a false dichotomy that does not necessarily entail a binary set of answers.
</details>

### Journal articles

'Philosophy as a Language-Game? An Appoach to Wittgenstein’s Philosophical Methodology' (2022). In: Jakub Mácha & Herbert Hrachovec (eds.), _Platonism: Contributions of the 43rd International Wittgenstein Symposium_. ALWS. [Published version.](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/404225835_Philosophy_as_a_Language-Game_An_Appoach_to_Wittgenstein's_Philosophical_Methodology)

<details>
<summary>A B S T R A C T</summary>
Wittgenstein introduces the notion of language-game as a methodological device, which aims to describe and clarify language use. This term allows us to regard language as internally related to action (PI 2009: 7). As Wittgenstein suggests, we can consider different human practices through the lens of this notion for clarificatory purposes (PI 2009: 5). This paper explores to what extent philosophy can represent a language-game. The first section presents the problem by showing that if philosophy has not articulated its own terminology, there would be no specific vocabulary related to a particular set of actions that constitutes the language-game of this practice. Section two expands on the notion of language-game and explains why science accords with this model in contrast to more mainstream philosophical approaches. The final section asks whether a Wittgensteinian approach to philosophy represents a language-game. By rejecting a binary answer to this question, this analysis will shed light on a crucial aspect of Wittgenstein’s philosophy, namely, that this approach can be understood as the practice that enables the understanding and facilitates the development of grammatically complex language-games (PI 2009: 125–126).
</details>

### MPhil Dissertation

'Wittgenstein and the Concept of Learning in Artificial Intelligence' (2020). [Published version.](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/404226021_Wittgenstein_and_the_Concept_of_Learning_in_Artificial_Intelligence)

<details>
<summary>A B S T R A C T</summary>
The object of this investigation is to analyze the application of the concept of learning to machines and software as displayed in Artiﬁcial Intelligence (AI). This ﬁeld has been approached from diﬀerent philosophical perspectives. AI, however, has not yet received enough attention from a Wittgensteinian angle, a gap this thesis aims to help bridge. First we describe the use of the concept of learning in natural language by means of a familiar and of a less familiar case of human learning. This is done to give us a general idea about the meaning of this concept. By building two basic machine learning algorithms, we introduce one of the technical meanings of “learning” in computer science, i.e. the use of this concept in machine learning. Based on a study and comparison between both uses, the one in ordinary language and the one in machine learning, we conclude that both usages exemplify one and the same “family resemblance” concept of learning. We apply this insight further in a critical discussion of two speciﬁc philosophical positions about the applicability of psychological or mental concepts to software and hardware, especially in AI. One of the contributions of this investigation is that the use of mental concepts concerning machines does not imply the ascription of a mind.
</details>
