I am a postgrad research student in Philosophy at the University of Southampton. My PhD is on the relation between psychological language and machine behaviour. 

I work under the supervision of [Genia Schönbaumsfeld](https://geniaschonbaumsfeld.academia.edu/) and [Will Mcneill](https://philpeople.org/profiles/william-e-s-mcneill). 

Since 2019 I am an editorial assistant in the _Nordic Wittgenstein Review_.

### Book chapters

'Wittgenstein, Psychological Language and Artificial Intelligence' (2024). In Brian Ball, Alice C. Helliwell, and Alessandro Rossi (eds.), _Wittgenstein and Artificial Intelligence, Volume I: Mind and Language_. Chapter 3. Anthem Press. [Published version.](https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/abs/wittgenstein-and-artificial-intelligence/wittgenstein-psychological-language-and-ai/CD66B9E88534ECED099FF1B400B2870F)

A B S T R A C T

> This chapter argues that certain uses of psychological language in the context of artificial intelligence (AI) escape the weak-strong AI dichotomy and can be understood in terms of, or as sharing features with, what Wittgenstein called the ‘secondary’ sense, meaning, or use of concepts.
The first part of the chapter reconstructs the fundamental problem in the philosophy of AI, namely, ‘Can machines think?’, and shows that figurative and literal uses of psychological language relate to the weak and strong conceptions of AI, respectively.
In the second part, we elaborate on the chapter’s argument. First, we explore the conceptual nature of the problem of ‘thinking’ machines. We then briefly review some Wittgensteinian approaches to these issues. Wittgenstein scholars have mainly criticised strong or literal ascriptions of psychological predicates to machines, and seem to agree that only weak or figurative uses of psychological language in AI are unproblematic.
In the last section we introduce a descriptive use of psychological language in the science of AI. We study such a use of language based on Wittgenstein’s methodological requirement of attending to and describing language use to clarify conceptual confusion. While showing how this use of language is meaningful in a secondary sense, we distinguish the descriptive usage from both literal and figurative ascriptions of psychological predicates to machines.
One of the outcomes of this study is that the analysis of meaningful uses of psychological language in AI in terms of ‘secondary use’ helps show that the problem ‘Can machines think?’ might give rise to a false dichotomy that does not necessarily entail a binary set of answers.
